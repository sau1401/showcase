GhostBSD

FreeBSD derivative for "beginners"

What is a "BSD"?

BSD, or Berkeley Software Distribution, is a now defunct operating system that was built on C and was a part of the Unix family (which would later offshot to modern-day Linux in the 90s). Many derivatives of this OS have popped up since its demise, including FreeBSD and OpenBSD, both of which are the "main" OSs that the remaining OSs are built off of.

Why GhostBSD?

Simple: it comes installed with "xfce", which is a GUI that most BSD derivatives do not have to boot. FreeBSD alone is GUI-less and you are stuck with a shell prompt to start, compared to Linux. Which brings me to the reason why I'm NOT interested in Linux...
While I am interested in understanding OSs closer to the kernel, Linux is a derivative the tries to make things more "user-friendly" rather than embracing what made Unix, well, Unix. Not to mention the instability of entirely open-sourced projects (not to say open-sourced projects are entirely bad). That, and due to most programs not even supporting FreeBSD/Unix, I will be forced to find programs that work and get creative with the bootstrapping/package installing processes.
My goal isn't to make BSD more favorable or less favorable, it's just to experience firsthand something that is more restricted/locked down. You are welcome to see my progress in this tedious yet thrilling task.

Running GhostBSD on a VM (Oracle VM)

Booting it up initially I had no issues; nothing wrong with the keyboard or any silly driver errors. I managed to set up my custom xface environment with a screensaver and all. However, rather than saving the state like VM users recommend, I just shut down the machine like a regular computer or just kept it idle in the background of my PC for days on end.
Unfortunately, this led to an issue where the machine couldn't boot after my PC crashed. The root was misplaced probably due to how it shutdown immediately, so even with my best attempts, I just had to reinstall the ISO and the OS as a whole. Learning my lessons now could seriously save me if my OS on my laptop goes haywire.
That aside, I am still trying to understand packets and various other things that can be installed on this more closed-off OS.
